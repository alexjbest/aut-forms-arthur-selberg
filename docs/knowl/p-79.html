A hyperbolic disk of radius \(R\) centred at \(i\) would be a euclidean disk, but not centered at \(i\text{.}\) The area is \(4\pi(\sinh (R/2))^2\) and the circumference is \(2\pi\sinh(R)\) these are roughly the same size as \(\sinh(x) = (e^x - e^{-x})/2\text{.}\) The euclidean area is far large (roughly the square) of the hyperbolic.
